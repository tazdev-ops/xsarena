# Linguistics (General & Historical/Comparative)

## Introduction to Historical Linguistics of Iranian Languages

Historical linguistics is the study of how languages change over time. When applied to Iranian languages, it involves understanding the evolution from **Proto-Indo-European** through **Proto-Iranian** to the various attested Iranian languages.

## The Indo-European Family and Iranian Branch

The **Indo-European family** includes most European languages plus several language families of Southwest, Central, and South Asia. The **Iranian branch** is part of the larger **Indo-Iranian** subfamily, which also includes the Indo-Aryan languages (Hindi, Sanskrit, etc.).

### Proto-Indo-European (PIE) to Proto-Iranian

**Proto-Indo-European** is the reconstructed common ancestor of all Indo-European languages, estimated to have been spoken around 4000-2500 BCE. The transition to **Proto-Iranian** involved several crucial sound changes:

1. **Satem change**: PIE palatovelars (*ḱ, *ǵ, *ǵʰ) became sibilants (*s) in Iranian (and other satem languages)
   - PIE *ḱm̥tóm > Proto-Iranian *satám > Old Persian *hatham* "hundred"

2. **Ruki rule**: Palatovelars became sibilants before *r, *u, *k, *i
   - PIE *ǵérh₂trom > Proto-Iranian *zaratram- > Avestan *zaraθra-* "golden"

3. **Deaffrication**: PIE affricates became fricatives in Iranian
   - PIE *ḱ > Proto-Iranian *s (before *r, *u, *k, *i)

## Sound Changes in Iranian Languages

### Iranian to Old Persian

The transition from Proto-Iranian to **Old Persian** shows several distinctive changes:

- **Sibilant strengthening**: Proto-Iranian *s > Old Persian clusters like *š (fricative)
- **Vowel reduction**: Loss of unstressed vowels in certain positions
- **Nasal loss**: In specific phonological environments
- **Consonant cluster simplification**: Particularly in word-final position

### Old Persian to Middle Persian (Pahlavi)

**Middle Persian** (Pahlavi) shows further evolution:

- **Vowel system changes**: Merging of some vowel distinctions
- **Consonant cluster reduction**: Further simplification of consonant clusters
- **Aramaic influence**: Through the adoption of the Pahlavi script

## Morphological Features

### Old Persian Morphology

Old Persian maintained many archaic Indo-European features:

- **Case system**: Nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, ablative, locative, instrumental
- **Number system**: Singular, dual, plural (dual preserved from PIE)
- **Voice system**: Active, middle, passive
- **Tense system**: Present, past (imperfect), perfect, aorist, future

### Middle Persian Simplification

Middle Persian shows significant morphological simplification:

- **Case system**: Reduction to basic subject-object-distinction
- **Number system**: Dual largely lost, singular-plural distinction maintained
- **Tense system**: Simplified to present-past-future
- **Agreement patterns**: Less complex than Old Persian

## Phonological Reconstruction

### Proto-Iranian Phonological System

**Consonant inventory** (simplified):
- Stops: p, t, k, č, kʷ, g, ǯ, b, d, ǧ, gʷ
- Affricates: ts, tš, dz, dž
- Fricatives: s, š, ž, h, hʷ
- Nasals: m, n, ŋ, ŋʷ
- Liquids: l, r
- Glides: w, y

**Vowel inventory**:
- Short: a, i, u
- Long: ā, ī, ū
- Diphthongs: various combinations

### Sound Change Laws

Several important sound change laws affected Iranian languages:

1. **Winter's Law**: Original PIE *h₁ > *j when followed by a vowel
2. **Grassmann's Law**: Aspiration dissimilation in clusters
3. **de Bruijn's Generalization**: Rules governing reduplication patterns

## Comparative Analysis: Avestan vs. Old Persian

While Avestan and Old Persian are closely related, they show important differences:

### Lexical Differences
- Avestan preserves more archaic vocabulary
- Old Persian shows more foreign loanwords (Elamite, Akkadian)
- Shared vocabulary shows regular sound correspondences

### Grammatical Differences
- Avestan preserves more case distinctions
- Old Persian shows more morphological innovations
- Different verbal system developments

## Syntactic Features

### Word Order
- Old Persian: Generally SOV (Subject-Object-Verb) with some VSO
- Middle Persian: Increasingly SOV
- Avestan: Both SOV and SVO patterns

### Clause Structure
- Relative clauses: Different formation patterns
- Subordination: Various strategies
- Coordination: Conjunction usage patterns

## Methodology of Historical Linguistics

### The Comparative Method
1. **Collection of data**: Gather cognate forms from related languages
2. **Establishment of correspondences**: Regular sound correspondences
3. **Reconstruction**: Reconstruct proto-language forms
4. **Internal reconstruction**: Further refinement using internal evidence

### Sound Correspondences Example
PIE *dóh₁tēr > Proto-Iranian *dugātar- >
- Avestan *dužatār*
- Old Persian *duštar*
- Middle Persian *dōšag*
- New Persian *doxtar*

## Teaching Notes

This section introduced the fundamental concepts of historical linguistics as applied to Iranian languages. Understanding sound change laws and the comparative method is essential for analyzing the relationships between different Iranian languages. The morphological changes from Old to Middle Persian represent a typical pattern of language simplification that students should recognize. The phonological reconstruction methods provide tools for understanding unattested stages of Iranian languages.
