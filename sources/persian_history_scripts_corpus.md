# Persian Language History & Scripts/Texts

## Introduction to Ancient Iranian Languages

Ancient Iranian languages form a branch of the Indo-Iranian language family, which itself belongs to the larger Indo-European family. These languages were spoken in the Iranian plateau and surrounding regions from approximately 2000 BCE to the present day.

## Old Persian

**Old Persian** was the primary language of the Achaemenid Empire (550-330 BCE) and is known primarily from royal inscriptions. It used a distinctive cuneiform script that was specifically adapted for Persian, different from the Mesopotamian cuneiform used for Sumerian and Akkadian.

### Old Persian Inscriptions

The most famous Old Persian inscription is the **Behistun Inscription** of Darius I, carved on a cliff face at Mount Behistun in western Iran around 520 BCE. This trilingual text (Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian) was crucial for the decipherment of Old Persian cuneiform, much like the Rosetta Stone was for Egyptian hieroglyphs.

Old Persian cuneiform consisted of about 40 signs, including syllabic signs and logographic signs. The script was written horizontally from left to right, and the language was characterized by:

- Simple vowel system (a, i, u)
- Complex consonant system with various stops and sibilants
- Nominative-accusative case system
- Three numbers (singular, dual, plural)

## Avestan

**Avestan** is the language of the Zoroastrian religious texts, the Avesta. It is closely related to Old Persian but shows more archaic features, suggesting it represents an earlier stage of development. Avestan is divided into two varieties:

- **Gathic Avestan**: The language of the hymns attributed to Zoroaster (Zarathushtra)
- **Younger Avestan**: The language of the remaining liturgical texts

## Middle Persian (Pahlavi)

Following Old Persian, **Middle Persian** or **Pahlavi** became the primary language of the Sasanian Empire (224-651 CE). Unlike Old Persian, Middle Persian was written in the **Pahlavi script**, which derived from Aramaic but was heavily adapted for Persian.

### Pahlavi Script & Heterograms

The **Pahlavi script** presented unique challenges for Persian text representation:

- **Heterograms** (Ar. *al-alfad al-mughayyara*): Aramaic words used to represent Persian words with similar sounds but different meanings
- **Haplography**: Writing one character where two would normally be expected
- **Contextual letter forms**: Aramaic letters modified to better represent Persian phonemes

Common heterograms included:
- MLKA for "king" (Aramaic: "melech")
- GBRʾ for "Magus" (Aramaic: "gabr")
- ʾBYN for "between" (Aramaic: "bein")

### Pahlavi Literature

Middle Persian literature includes important Zoroastrian texts and secular works:

- **Bundahishn**: "Primal Creation," a Zoroastrian account of creation and cosmogony
- **Dēnkard**: "Acts of the Religion," an encyclopedic work of Zoroastrian doctrine
- **Karnamag i Ardashir i Pabagan**: The legendary history of Ardashir I, founder of the Sasanian dynasty

## Sound Changes from Proto-Iranian

Several significant sound changes distinguish Old Persian from Proto-Iranian:

1. **Satemization**: Preservation of palatal consonants (e.g., Proto-Indo-European *ḱ > Old Persian *c*)
2. **Vowel reduction**: Loss of unstressed vowels in certain positions
3. **Consonant cluster simplification**: Reduction of complex consonant clusters
4. **Metathesis**: Reversal of sound order in certain morphemes
